The Internet

The Internet began in the 1960s as a way for government researchers to share information. Computers in the 1960s were large and immobile,
and to make use of the information stored in any one computer, one had to either travel to the computer's location or send the computer's
magnetic tapes through the traditional postal system.
Internet is a world-wide global system of interconnected computer networks.Internet uses the standard Internet Protocol TCP/IP.
Every computer in internet is identified by aunique IP address.IP Address is a unique set of numbers such as 110.22.33.114 which identifies a
computer location.
A computer's private DNS domain name server is used to give a name to an IP address so that a user can locate a computer by name.
For example, the DNS server will resolve the name http://www.yahoo.com to a specific IP address to uniquely identify the computer on which the website is hosted.
The Internet is available to every user all over the world. The Internet began in the United States more than 50 years ago as a government weapon in the Cold War.
For years, scientists and researchers have used it to communicate and share data with each other. Today, we use the Internet for almost everything, and for many people,
it would be impossible to imagine life without it. Another catalyst in shaping the Internet was the outbreak of the Cold War. The Soviet launch of Sputnik prompted the
US Department of Defense to consider ways in which information could be released even after a nuclear attack. This eventually led to the formation of the ARPANET
(Advance Research Projects Agency Network), the network that eventually evolved into what we now know as the Internet. ARPANET has been a huge success,
but membership is limited to some academic and research institutions that have contracts with the Department of Defense.
In response, other networks were created to provide information exchange.
January 1, 1983 is considered the official birthday of the Internet. Before that, different computer networks did not have a standard way to communicate with each other.
A new communications protocol called Transport Control Protocol/Working over the Internet TCP/IP has been created.
This allowed different types of computers on different networks to "talk" to each other. ARPANET and the Defense Data Network were officially changed to the TCP/IP
standard on January 1, 1983, hence the birth of the Internet. All networks can now be linked into a global language.

Who invented the internet?

The Internet was not invented by anyone. When networking technology was first developed, a few scientists and engineers pooled their research together to create the ARPANET.
Later, the innovations of other inventors paved the way for the Web as we know it today.

PAUL BARAN (1926-2011)
An engineer whose work overlapped with ARPA research. In 1959, he joined an American think-tank, the RAND Corporation, and was asked to research how the US Air Force would
control its fleet in the event of a nuclear attack. In 1964 Baran proposed a communications network without a central command point. If one point is destroyed, all remaining points will
still be able to communicate with each other. This is called a distributed network.


LAWRENCE ROBERTS (1937-2018)
Chief Scientist at ARPA, responsible for developing computer networks. Paul Baran's idea impressed Roberts, and work began on a distributed network.


BOB KAHN (1938-) AND VINT CERF (1943-)
American computer scientists who developed TCP/IP, the set of protocols that govern how data travels across a network. This helped the ARPANET evolve into the Internet we use today.
Vint Cerf is credited with the first written use of the word "internet". When asked to explain my role in creating the Internet, I generally use the example of a city.
I helped build roads - the infrastructure that gets things from point A to point B.
